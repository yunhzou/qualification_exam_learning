\subsection{Why outcome-only GRPO becomes insufficient}

GRPO improves reasoning when completed outcomes can be verified reliably, as demonstrated
by DeepSeek-R1 \citep{guo2025deepseekr1}. Its advantage nevertheless remains one number
for the whole trajectory. It cannot distinguish a decisive reasoning step from irrelevant
tokens, prompts with uniformly failed samples provide no relative signal, and imperfect
verifiers can reward shortcuts. Longer trajectories amplify these limitations while also
increasing sequential rollout cost.

Agent systems add a second problem: the optimized tokens must remain attached to the policy
version and exact context that generated them. Asynchronous workers create policy lag;
tool responses are context rather than policy actions; context replacement, subagents, and
parallel branches can require new trace boundaries rather than one fictional continuous
trajectory \citep{glm5team2026,xu2026polar}. These limits motivate the later comparison:
process reward models and critics attempt to localize progress, while on-policy
distillation supplies dense teacher feedback on the student's encountered states. They are
not merely alternative optimizers; they add information that terminal group comparison
does not contain.
