\subsection{From expected advantage to the GRPO reward surrogate}
\label{subsec:grpo-importance-ratio}

Begin with the parent objective in Equation~\ref{eq:grpo-parent-expected-advantage}. For a
fixed prompt, rewrite its expectation using trajectories sampled by the behavior policy:
\begin{align}
  J_{\mathrm{adv}}(\theta)
  &=\sum_o \pi_\theta(o\mid q)A_{\mathrm{old}}(q,o) \notag\\
  &=\sum_o \pi_{\mathrm{old}}(o\mid q)
    \frac{\pi_\theta(o\mid q)}{\pi_{\mathrm{old}}(o\mid q)}
    A_{\mathrm{old}}(q,o) \notag\\
  &=\mathbb{E}_{o\sim\pi_{\mathrm{old}}}
    \left[w_i(\theta)A_i\right],
  \qquad
  w_i(\theta)=\frac{\pi_\theta(o_i\mid q)}
                    {\pi_{\mathrm{old}}(o_i\mid q)}.
  \label{eq:grpo-trajectory-importance-expectation}
\end{align}
This equality is ordinary importance sampling. It changes the distribution used to compute
the expectation without changing the expectation itself. With $G$ stored trajectories, a
Monte Carlo estimate of this exact expression is
\begin{equation}
  \widehat J_{\mathrm{trajectory}}(\theta)
  =\frac{1}{G}\sum_{i=1}^{G}w_i(\theta)A_i.
  \label{eq:grpo-trajectory-importance-estimator}
\end{equation}

For an autoregressive response, the trajectory ratio is a product of token ratios:
\begin{equation}
  w_i(\theta)
  =\prod_{t=1}^{T_i}\rho_{i,t}(\theta),
  \qquad
  \rho_{i,t}(\theta)
  =\frac{\pi_\theta(o_{i,t}\mid q,o_{i,<t})}
         {\pi_{\mathrm{old}}(o_{i,t}\mid q,o_{i,<t})}.
  \label{eq:grpo-complete-trajectory-ratio}
\end{equation}
Up to this point, the connection to the parent objective is exact. The difficulty is that
a product of many ratios can become extremely small or large, so its variance grows badly
with response length.

GRPO therefore makes a deliberate surrogate choice: it replaces the single trajectory
product with an average of local per-token ratios while broadcasting the trajectory
advantage to every sampled token:
\begin{equation}
  J_{\mathrm{reward}}^{\mathrm{GRPO}}(\theta)
  =\frac{1}{G}\sum_{i=1}^{G}\frac{1}{T_i}\sum_{t=1}^{T_i}
    \rho_{i,t}(\theta)A_i.
  \label{eq:grpo-unclipped-reward-objective}
\end{equation}
Equation~\ref{eq:grpo-unclipped-reward-objective} is not algebraically equal to the exact
trajectory estimator in Equation~\ref{eq:grpo-trajectory-importance-estimator}. It is a
local policy-improvement surrogate chosen to avoid the unstable trajectory-ratio product.
This is the unclipped reward-driven term that appears inside
Equation~\ref{eq:grpo-full-loss}.

The surrogate still produces the intended policy-gradient direction near the behavior
policy. Because
\begin{equation}
  \nabla_\theta\rho_{i,t}
  =\rho_{i,t}\nabla_\theta\log\pi_\theta(o_{i,t}\mid q,o_{i,<t}),
  \label{eq:grpo-ratio-gradient}
\end{equation}
and $\rho_{i,t}=1$ when $\pi_\theta=\pi_{\mathrm{old}}$, its initial gradient is
\begin{equation}
  \left.\nabla_\theta J_{\mathrm{reward}}^{\mathrm{GRPO}}\right|_{\pi_\theta=\pi_{\mathrm{old}}}
  =\frac{1}{G}\sum_{i=1}^{G}\frac{A_i}{T_i}\sum_{t=1}^{T_i}
    \nabla_\theta\log\pi_\theta(o_{i,t}\mid q,o_{i,<t}).
  \label{eq:grpo-advantage-weighted-gradient}
\end{equation}
A positive $A_i$ therefore increases the log-probabilities of the sampled actions, while
a negative $A_i$ decreases them. For a positive-advantage response `5 <eos>', both the
decision to emit `5' and the decision to stop after `5' are reinforced. The factor $1/T_i$
gives each response equal total normalization rather than automatically giving longer
responses more weight.

The derivation has now reached the $\rho_{i,t}A_i$ term in the full GRPO formula. The
remaining modifications are stabilizers: Section~\ref{subsec:grpo-clipped-objective}
clips this reward surrogate and subtracts the reference-policy KL cost before converting
the maximization objective into the minimized loss in
Equation~\ref{eq:grpo-full-loss}.
