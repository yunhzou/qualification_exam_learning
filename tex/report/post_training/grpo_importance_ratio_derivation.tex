\subsection{From stored behavior to the GRPO reward surrogate}
\label{subsec:grpo-importance-ratio}

The learner wants to ask how well the new policy would perform, but its expensive stored
responses were sampled by the old policy. A response that became twice as probable under
the new policy should count twice as much when estimating the new expectation; one that
became half as probable should count half as much. The new-to-old probability ratio makes
exactly this change of perspective.

For a fixed prompt, inserting that ratio rewrites the desired expected advantage without
changing its value:
\begin{align}
  J_{\mathrm{adv}}(\theta)
  &=\sum_o \pi_\theta(o\mid q)A_{\mathrm{old}}(q,o) \notag\\
  &=\sum_o \pi_{\mathrm{old}}(o\mid q)
    \frac{\pi_\theta(o\mid q)}{\pi_{\mathrm{old}}(o\mid q)}
    A_{\mathrm{old}}(q,o) \notag\\
  &=\mathbb{E}_{o\sim\pi_{\mathrm{old}}}
    \left[w_i(\theta)A_i\right],
  \qquad
  w_i(\theta)=\frac{\pi_\theta(o_i\mid q)}
                    {\pi_{\mathrm{old}}(o_i\mid q)}.
  \label{eq:grpo-trajectory-importance-expectation}
\end{align}
With $G$ stored responses, the corresponding Monte Carlo estimate is
\begin{equation}
  \widehat J_{\mathrm{trajectory}}(\theta)
  =\frac{1}{G}\sum_{i=1}^{G}w_i(\theta)A_i.
  \label{eq:grpo-trajectory-importance-estimator}
\end{equation}
This trajectory-level importance correction is still exactly connected to the original
expected-advantage objective.

The next problem comes from response length. An autoregressive response probability is a
product, so the exact response ratio is also a product:
\begin{equation}
  w_i(\theta)
  =\prod_{t=1}^{T_i}\rho_{i,t}(\theta),
  \qquad
  \rho_{i,t}(\theta)
  =\frac{\pi_\theta(o_{i,t}\mid q,o_{i,<t})}
         {\pi_{\mathrm{old}}(o_{i,t}\mid q,o_{i,<t})}.
  \label{eq:grpo-complete-trajectory-ratio}
\end{equation}
Multiplying hundreds or thousands of slightly imperfect ratios can produce an extremely
small or large number. One entire response then receives almost no weight or dominates
the batch, making the exact estimator too unstable for a practical local update.

GRPO chooses a more local question: for each sampled token decision, did the new policy
make that same decision more or less likely? It averages those local ratios while giving
every token the response's shared advantage:
\begin{equation}
  J_{\mathrm{reward}}^{\mathrm{GRPO}}(\theta)
  =\frac{1}{G}\sum_{i=1}^{G}\frac{1}{T_i}\sum_{t=1}^{T_i}
    \rho_{i,t}(\theta)A_i.
  \label{eq:grpo-unclipped-reward-objective}
\end{equation}
This is the crucial non-equivalence. Equation~\ref{eq:grpo-unclipped-reward-objective} is
not another exact rewrite of Equation~\ref{eq:grpo-trajectory-importance-estimator}; it is
GRPO's local surrogate for avoiding the high-variance trajectory product. The factor
$1/T_i$ gives each response equal total normalization rather than letting a longer answer
win merely by containing more token terms.

Near the behavior policy, this surrogate still implements the intended ``good up, bad
down'' update. Initially every local ratio is one, but its gradient is not zero:
\begin{equation}
  \nabla_\theta\rho_{i,t}
  =\rho_{i,t}\nabla_\theta\log\pi_\theta(o_{i,t}\mid q,o_{i,<t}).
  \label{eq:grpo-ratio-gradient}
\end{equation}
Therefore its initial gradient is the advantage-weighted log-probability gradient derived
from intuition in Section~\ref{subsec:grpo-gradient-direction}:
\begin{equation}
  \left.\nabla_\theta J_{\mathrm{reward}}^{\mathrm{GRPO}}\right|_{\pi_\theta=\pi_{\mathrm{old}}}
  =\frac{1}{G}\sum_{i=1}^{G}\frac{A_i}{T_i}\sum_{t=1}^{T_i}
    \nabla_\theta\log\pi_\theta(o_{i,t}\mid q,o_{i,<t}).
  \label{eq:grpo-advantage-weighted-gradient}
\end{equation}
The reward-driven part of the full formula has now emerged. What remains is to stop stale
samples from supporting excessively large updates and to protect behavior not measured by
the reward.
